# Step A — Short Story

**Parameters:** Mauritania 🇲🇷 | Grapes 🍇 | Bee 🐝 | Flying 🧑‍✈️ | Haunted House 🏚️

---

In the sun-scorched outskirts of Atar, Mauritania, where the Sahara creeps into abandoned French colonial estates, a young drone pilot named Aminata discovered that the crumbling Maison Gris — a stone villa locals swore was haunted by the ghost of a colonial vintner — was in fact alive, not with spirits, but with the largest wild bee colony anyone in the Adrar region had ever seen. The bees had colonised the walls, the rafters, the collapsed wine cellar where desiccated grape vines still clung to rusted trellises, building a vast labyrinth of comb that hummed so deeply the village children said the house was breathing. Aminata, who earned her living flying agricultural survey drones for a French-Mauritanian development NGO, had been mapping abandoned structures for a solar panel siting project when her thermal camera revealed the swarm — tens of thousands of Saharan honeybees thriving in a microclimate created by the villa's thick stone walls and the residual moisture of an underground spring that had once irrigated the vintner's grape terraces. She returned the next dawn with her mentor, old Ousmane, a retired beekeeper who had lost his last hives to drought five years earlier, and together they watched through the drone's live feed as bees spiralled from every window and crack, flying out across the hammada in disciplined foraging lines toward a wadi where, impossibly, wild grape hybrids still fruited in the shade of acacia trees. Ousmane wept — not because the house was haunted, but because it proved what he had always argued and what the development agencies had always dismissed: that traditional desert apiculture, paired with drought-resistant viticulture, could transform abandoned colonial ruins into productive agricultural hubs, if only someone with the technology to see from above would bother to look.
