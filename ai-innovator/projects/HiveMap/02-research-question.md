## Research Question

**To what extent can drone-based remote sensing, combined with traditional apicultural knowledge, enable the identification and rehabilitation of abandoned agricultural infrastructure for smallholder agroforestry in arid Sub-Saharan regions — and what are the socioeconomic and ecological conditions under which such interventions become self-sustaining?**

### Academic Framing

This question sits at the intersection of **precision agriculture**, **post-colonial land reuse**, and **climate-adaptive livelihoods** in the Sahel. It draws on:

- **Remote sensing and UAV applications in agriculture** — the use of thermal and multispectral drone imaging to identify viable microclimates and wild pollinator populations in degraded landscapes (Matese et al., 2015; Tmušić et al., 2020).
- **Desert apiculture and pollinator ecology** — the capacity of Saharan bee populations (*Apis mellifera sahariensis*) to sustain honey production and crop pollination in extreme heat, and the indigenous knowledge systems that managed them before colonial and post-colonial disruption (Hepburn and Radloff, 2011).
- **Drought-resistant viticulture** — the agronomic viability of hybrid grape cultivars in arid conditions, particularly those descended from colonial-era plantings that have undergone natural selection for heat and water stress tolerance (This et al., 2006).
- **Adaptive reuse of colonial infrastructure** — the policy and economic frameworks for repurposing abandoned built environments in former colonies for productive use, including land tenure, heritage protection, and community ownership models (Njoh, 2009).

### Discipline

Agricultural Technology / Development Studies / Environmental Science

### Suitability

This question is specific (drone-enabled identification of rehabilitation sites), researchable (testable via pilot deployments and yield data), and grounded in real-world development challenges across the Sahel region.
