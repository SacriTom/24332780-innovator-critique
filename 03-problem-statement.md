## The Problem

Across the Sahel belt — Mauritania, Mali, Senegal, Burkina Faso, Niger, and Chad — an estimated **12 million hectares of formerly productive agricultural land** have been abandoned over the past four decades due to desertification, rural-to-urban migration, and the collapse of colonial-era farming systems (UNCCD, 2022). Within this vast degraded landscape, **thousands of abandoned structures** (colonial estates, government agricultural stations, traditional stone compounds) create unrecognised microclimates: their thick walls retain moisture, their ruins shelter wild pollinator colonies, and their proximity to underground water sources sustains remnant vegetation — including wild grape hybrids and acacia groves — that could anchor productive agroforestry systems.

**The problem is not a lack of viable land — it is a lack of visibility.** These micro-opportunities are invisible from the ground and from satellite imagery. They require low-altitude, high-resolution sensing (thermal, multispectral, LiDAR) that only drone technology can provide at an affordable cost. Meanwhile, the **indigenous knowledge** needed to interpret what the drones find — which bee species indicate water, which soil crusts signal fertility, which ruins can be structurally reused — resides with ageing traditional farmers and beekeepers who are excluded from the technology-driven development conversation.

## Target Customer Segment

**Smallholder farming cooperatives in arid Sub-Saharan Africa** (specifically Mauritania's Adrar and Tagant regions), comprising 50–200 member households, who:

- Currently farm marginal rain-fed plots yielding below subsistence thresholds
- Have access to abandoned infrastructure within 20 km of their communities
- Include or are adjacent to traditional beekeeping knowledge holders
- Are served by at least one development NGO or government agricultural extension programme

**Estimated segment size:** ~3,400 cooperatives across Mauritania alone (~180,000 households), with a broader Sahel-wide addressable segment of ~28,000 cooperatives.

## Why This Problem Persists

1. **Technology mismatch:** Satellite imagery (the default tool for development agencies) lacks the resolution to identify building-scale microclimates. Drone surveys exist but are deployed for mining and infrastructure, not smallholder agriculture.
2. **Knowledge silos:** Traditional ecological knowledge (TEK) holders and drone operators work in entirely separate ecosystems with no shared framework or incentive to collaborate.
3. **Funding blind spots:** Climate adaptation funding flows to large-scale reforestation (e.g., the Great Green Wall) and ignores micro-scale, structure-based rehabilitation that could deliver faster, cheaper, more localised results.
4. **Land tenure ambiguity:** Abandoned colonial structures exist in a legal grey zone — neither formally state-owned nor community-claimed — creating risk for anyone who invests in rehabilitating them.

## The Opportunity

A platform that **combines drone-based environmental sensing with traditional ecological knowledge** to systematically identify, score, and prioritise abandoned sites for agroforestry rehabilitation — turning invisible micro-assets into bankable, investment-ready agricultural projects for smallholder cooperatives.
